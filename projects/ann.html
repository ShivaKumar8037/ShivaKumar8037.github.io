Mortality Prediction in ICU

  • Tech Stack: Python and various Python libraries: Pandas, Seaborn, Matplotlib, etc..
  • Github URL: Project Link

During my time as an undergraduate student, I was deeply interested in the intersection of healthcare and technology. Recognizing the potential of machine learning in healthcare, I decided to undertake a significant project that aimed to predict mortality rates in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU). The ICU is a critical environment where patients are under constant monitoring due to the severity of their conditions. Predicting mortality rates in such settings can be of immense value, as it can assist healthcare professionals in making informed decisions, optimizing resource allocation, and potentially improving patient outcomes. For this project, I chose to employ an Artificial Neural Network (ANN) â€“ a type of machine learning model inspired by the structure and functional aspects of biological neural networks. ANNs are particularly adept at handling vast amounts of data, recognizing patterns, and making predictions, making them an apt choice for this task. The process began with data collection. We sourced patient data from a reputable hospital, ensuring all data was anonymized to maintain patient confidentiality. This dataset included various parameters like vital signs, lab results, medical history, and more. After cleaning and preprocessing the data to ensure its quality, we split it into training and testing sets. Next, I designed the ANN architecture, considering various layers, nodes, and activation functions to optimize its performance. Training the model involved feeding it the training data and adjusting the weights based on the error in predictions. Over time, the ANN was able to recognize patterns and correlations within the data that were indicative of a patient's likelihood of survival. Post-training, we evaluated the model using the testing set to gauge its accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity. The results were promising, with the ANN achieving a high degree of accuracy in predicting mortality rates. However, like all models, it wasn't infallible and had certain limitations, which we documented. Throughout the project, I collaborated with medical professionals to ensure the model's predictions were clinically relevant. Their insights were invaluable in tweaking the model and understanding its real-world implications. In conclusion, this project was not only a testament to the capabilities of ANNs but also highlighted the potential of machine learning in revolutionizing healthcare. While the model needs further refinement and validation on larger datasets, the initial results are a promising step towards a future where technology aids in enhancing patient care in the ICU.
